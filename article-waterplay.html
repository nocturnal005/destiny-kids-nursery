Water Play as a Vehicle for Emotional Consistency in Early Childhood Education

✍️ Destiny Kids Nursery 📅 12 April 2026 ⏱ 4 min read

Introduction

Emotional regulation remains one of the most complex developmental challenges in early childhood education. A growing body of peer-reviewed research now points to structured water play as an effective, evidence-based intervention for supporting emotional consistency in young children, with implications for nursery school practice globally.

A classroom action research study conducted by Irawati, Rouf, and Syarif (2026) at Muhammadiyah University Cirebon, Indonesia, examined the impact of water play activities on the emotional development of early childhood learners. The study, published in the Journal of Educational Sciences, found that sensory-based water play activities created conditions in which children practised patience, adhered to structured rules, and engaged in positive peer interaction — all of which are foundational components of social-emotional competence (Irawati et al., 2026).

Critically, the research identified that the calming properties of water as a sensory medium are not incidental. Rather, they serve a deliberate pedagogical function. The tactile and repetitive nature of water play provides children with a regulated sensory environment that reduces emotional reactivity and supports the development of self-control (Irawati et al., 2026). This is particularly significant for nursery-age children, many of whom lack the neurological maturity to manage emotional responses through cognitive strategies alone.

The study's findings align with broader scholarship on sensory-based interventions in early childhood settings, which consistently identifies physical, play-mediated engagement as more developmentally effective than passive or screen-based alternatives for emotional skill-building (Finkel, 2026).

Conclusion

The evidence presented by Irawati et al. (2026) makes a compelling case for the deliberate integration of water play into nursery school curricula. Institutions that treat sensory play as a structured pedagogical tool — rather than recreational filler — are better positioned to support children's long-term emotional competence, social readiness, and overall developmental outcomes.
